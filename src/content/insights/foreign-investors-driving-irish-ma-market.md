Ireland's mergers and acquisitions (M&A) market is thriving, with foreign investors driving a significant portion of deal activity. In 2024 alone, overseas buyers were behind 239 of the 462 completed transactions — a clear sign that Ireland remains a key destination for international investment.

Ireland's strong, stable economy, well-established financial services sector, and position as a gateway to the EU make it an attractive market. However, the deal-making process for those looking to enter is becoming increasingly complex. For foreign investors, success in the Irish market isn't just about finding the right target — it's about understanding the local business environment, navigating regulations, and structuring deals correctly. That's where having a trusted advisor on the ground becomes invaluable.

## Local expertise, global strength

"As part of HLB International, we're connected to a global network of dealmakers, sector specialists, and advisors in over 150 countries," says George Hardie, Corporate Finance Director at HLB Ireland. "That gives our clients the best of both worlds — local insight combined with international expertise." HLB Ireland works closely with overseas buyers, helping them assess opportunities, structure deals and navigate the Irish regulatory landscape.

## Why local advisors are essential in Irish M&A

**Market knowledge and strong networks.** "Ireland's M&A market is highly relationship-driven," says George. "Having a local advisor means access to key market insights and connections with investors, legal professionals and regulators. These relationships can be the difference between a deal stalling and closing successfully."

**Navigating Irish regulations and compliance.** Ireland has strict corporate governance, tax laws and competition rules that must be carefully considered in any transaction. "Foreign investors are often surprised by how much local regulatory nuances can impact a deal," George explains. "Understanding these frameworks upfront can help avoid delays, unexpected costs, or even deal failure."
